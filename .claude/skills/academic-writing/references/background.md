# Background

The background section proves the writer understands the problem domain. After reading it, the reader should trust the writer's competence and have enough context to evaluate the design and findings that follow. This is not related work — it provides context for the problem, not a survey of every paper in the space.

## Required Elements

Missing any element is a fail.

- [ ] The fundamental problem domain explained from first principles. What is the core concept the reader must grasp before any technology or method is introduced?
- [ ] The approach the work actually uses, covered in enough depth that the reader understands how it works and why it fits the problem.
- [ ] Alternative approaches that exist in the same space. Each named, and explained enough to explain why we did not choose to go with their approaches. Each topic individually is not explained in detail.
- [ ] What remains unsolved or unaddressed that this paper targets. The bridge from background knowledge to the contribution.

## Rules

Each is pass/fail.

1. Present the core principle before any specific technology or method. The reader must understand what problem exists before learning about any tool that addresses it. 

2. Everything must serve the writer's specific problem. If a paragraph could appear unchanged in a general-purpose textbook or reference manual, it is too broad. Every sentence earns its place by connecting to the writer's research question.

3. Every reference must connect to a narrative thread that leads to the writer's problem. Papers are not listed sequentially or summarized in isolation. A citation appears because it supports, contradicts, or refines a point in the argument the writer is building.

4. Individual papers do not get deep treatment. The focus is context, not survey. If a paper needs extended discussion to position within the narrative, the writer is drifting into related work.

5. An alternative method gets one to two sentences: name it, state why it does not fit, move on. Do not explain how the alternative works internally.

6. Follow the ordering: fundamentals of the domain first, then the chosen approach in depth, then alternatives briefly dismissed, then the gap. This is the only ordering that lets the reader accumulate knowledge without forward references.

7. Demonstrate understanding without overexplaining. Write enough that the reader can follow the problem and evaluate the work. Conciseness signals mastery — padding signals insecurity.
