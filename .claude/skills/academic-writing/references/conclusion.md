# Conclusion

The conclusion is the final impression the reader takes away. It synthesizes — it does not summarize. The reader has already seen the details; this chapter distills them into the clearest possible statement of what was done, what was found, and why it matters.

The focus is synthesis, not repetition. Compress the work into its essence and leave the reader with a clear takeaway.

## Required Elements

Missing any element is a fail.

- [ ] What problem did this work address and what was the contribution? Restate both in compressed form. The reader should be able to understand the purpose and outcome of the entire paper from this alone.
- [ ] What were the key findings? State only the results that matter most — the ones that directly answer the motivating question. This is not a list of every measurement.
- [ ] What do the findings mean beyond this paper? State the practical or theoretical implications for the domain. The reader must leave knowing why this work matters to them.
- [ ] What comes next? Name the most important open question or direction that follows from this work.

## Rules

Each is pass/fail.

1. Do not introduce new arguments, evidence, or findings. Everything in the conclusion must have been established earlier. If a point appears here for the first time, it belongs in an earlier chapter.

2. Do not restate the paper section by section. "Chapter 2 covered background, Chapter 3 described the design..." is a table of contents, not a conclusion. Synthesize across sections — state what the work achieved as a whole.

3. Keep it short. The conclusion is the most compressed chapter. If it runs longer than a few paragraphs, material is being re-discussed rather than synthesized. Every sentence must earn its place.

4. State findings in terms of the original problem, not in terms of the system. "The system achieved 180 ms discovery latency" is a result. "Services can be discovered fast enough for interactive use without requiring a central registry" is a conclusion. Write the latter.

5. Implications must be concrete. "These results have implications for the field" is filler — it could end any paper. "Network administrators deploying local services on networks under 5,000 nodes can use mDNS without infrastructure changes" gives the reader something actionable.

6. Future work must be selective. Name one or two directions that follow most naturally from the findings. A long wish list of unrelated possibilities signals that the author has not thought critically about what matters most.

7. Do not undermine the work. Acknowledging limitations is necessary in the discussion, but the conclusion is not the place to dwell on what went wrong. State what was accomplished with confidence.

8. Connect back to the opening motivation. The introduction established why the problem matters; the conclusion should echo that framing. The reader should feel the arc from problem to resolution.

9. End on the strongest possible statement. The final sentence is the last thing the reader carries away. Make it count — close with the significance of the contribution or the most important open question, not with a procedural remark or a generic call for further study.
