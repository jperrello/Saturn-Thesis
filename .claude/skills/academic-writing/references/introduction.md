# Introduction

The introduction should provide the reader with an understanding of their problem, why the problem matters, and what this work contributes. The introduction is not a summary of the paper — it is the primaryargument for why the paper needs to exist.

The focus is rhetorical, not informational. Establish territory, carve out a niche, and claim it. This is where your claims and arguments should be made.

## Required Elements

Missing any element is a fail.

- [ ] The broader problem domain grounded in concrete terms. What real situation or technical reality creates the conditions for this work? The reader must see the world the problem lives in before seeing the problem itself.
- [ ] The specific gap, tension, or unsolved question within that domain. This is the instability that justifies the work. Name it directly — do not gesture at it.
- [ ] What this work contributes, stated as an outcome. The reader must know what was built, found, or demonstrated — not what the author intended to explore.
- [ ] A brief preview of how the rest of the paper is organized. One sentence per major section is sufficient.

## Rules

Each is pass/fail.

1. Open with the problem domain, not the field. "Local networks depend on discovery protocols to find services" passes. "Computer networking is an important area of research" fails. The first sentence must place the reader inside a concrete situation.

2. Do not throat-clear. "In recent years," "It is well known that," "Since the dawn of time," and any other generic opener that could introduce any paper is filler. Cut it.

3. Narrow from broad to specific. Present the domain first, then the gap within it, then the contribution that addresses the gap. Never reverse this order. The reader accumulates context sentence by sentence — a forward reference to something not yet established forces them to reread.

4. State contributions as outcomes, not intentions. "We aim to explore," "This paper investigates," and "We set out to examine" describe plans, not results. "We built a system that resolves services in under 200 ms" describes an outcome. The introduction is written after the work is done — write accordingly.

5. The gap must be falsifiable or concrete. "Little research has been done" is vague and unverifiable. "No existing protocol handles networks above 5,000 nodes without a central registry" is specific and checkable. If the gap cannot be stated precisely, the contribution cannot be evaluated.

6. Do not over-explain background. Provide only enough context for the reader to understand the gap. If a concept needs more than two sentences of explanation, it belongs in a dedicated section, not here.

7. Every claim about the domain must attach to a reference. The introduction builds credibility by showing command of existing work. An unsupported claim about the state of the field undermines that credibility immediately.

8. The structure preview must be mechanical, not promotional. "Section 3 describes the system design" passes. "Section 3 presents our innovative design" fails. The preview tells the reader where things are, not how to feel about them.

9. Do not list contributions as bullet points. Weave them into the argument. A bulleted contribution list breaks the rhetorical flow and reads as a grant proposal, not a finished paper.

10. Close the introduction on the contribution, not on the structure preview. The last substantive statement the reader encounters should be what this work achieves. The preview, if included, is a navigational aid — not the climax.
