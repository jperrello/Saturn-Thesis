# Writing Notes

Raw ideas and examples for the General Writing Principles section of the academic writing skill.

---

## Clarity Through Concreteness

The best way to sound smart is to be clear, concrete, and direct. When the reader understands exactly what you mean, they view you as educated. Going out of your way to use abstractions or "big words" usually just confuses.

Unnecessary abstraction makes things vague. Compare:

- (a) The bully punched the small kid in the arm.
- (b) The bully behaved aggressively toward the small kid.

(a) tells me exactly what happened. (b) could mean yelling, insulting, threatening, throwing things. Use abstract terms for abstract things, concrete terms for concrete things.

Gain clarity by:

- Breaking sentences into easier-to-understand pieces. Simplify. Cut excess nouns, adjectives, adverbs.
- Using active verbs attached to clearly identified actors. Minimize "was," "were," "is."
- Avoiding nominalizations — actions expressed as nouns. Examples: "argument" instead of "argue," "analysis" instead of "analyze," "performance" instead of "perform." Flip these back into verbs.

## Reader-Centered Writing

The writing is NOT about the author. It's about the reader. Don't try to show the reader something about your thought process (that you thought through a topic, that you have something smart to say). The reader doesn't care. They care whether you provide something valuable to THEM.

Who reads academic work? Experts in the discipline, sometimes the general public. Why? Because they expect valuable information or ideas. If they don't find value quickly, they stop reading. So: make arguments, challenge the reader, offer value directly, be entertaining.

Organize for the reader's benefit. This requires empathy — imagine yourself in the reader's shoes. Ask: Who is my reader? What do they already know? What must I explain? What order prevents confusion? Where are the likely points of confusion that need extra care?

## Dialogic Framing

Writing is dialogic. You don't write in isolation — you enter a conversation, a debate, a controversy. First understand and identify an existing debate, then intervene and contribute.

Frame your writing as a solution to a problem readers perceive. This creates instability that makes your work valuable. Grab attention: debunk a myth, correct a misconception, enter a debate, challenge a popular interpretation. Be prepared to explain the significance of your topic succinctly and compellingly.

Template: "In discussions of ____, controversy has swirled around _____."

## Structural Mechanics

Be careful about paragraph beginnings and endings — these are natural emphasis points.

Use transition phrases and words to guide the reader:

- Multiple reasons: In addition, Also, In the first case
- Explain: Because, Given, Since
- Conclude: Therefore, Hence, Consequently
- Illustrate: A case in point, To illustrate
- Specific example: Specifically, Namely
- Intensify: Above all, Moreover, Furthermore, More importantly
- Emphasize: Of course, Indeed, Certainly
- Compare: Similarly, Likewise
- Contrast: However, On the other hand, Even so
- Speculate: Let's assume, Let's suppose
- Concede: Of course, Doubtless, While recognizing that...

Transitional phrases for structure:
- I will begin by...
- Before I say what is wrong with..., I will first...
- At this point, we need to consider the following objection...
- Although I have shown..., I still need to...
- Next, I will offer support for what is perhaps my most controversial claim, that...
- Having argued that..., I need to consider rival views...
